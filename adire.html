Ancient Techniques

Originating from Abeokuta in southwestern Nigeria, Adire—which literally means "tie and dye" in Yoruba—represents one of Africa's most sophisticated resist-dyeing traditions.

Adire Oniko

Raffia ties create organic, circular patterns symbolizing unity and the cyclical nature of life

Adire Alabere

Delicate stitching produces intricate linear designs requiring immense patience and skill

Adire Eleko

Cassava paste painted with feathers or stencils forms elaborate, story-telling motifs

Each technique requires days of meticulous work, with fabrics dipped repeatedly into fermented indigo vats until they achieve that signature deep, luminous blue.

The Women of Indigo

Adire has always been women's work—a matrilineal heritage passed from grandmother to daughter, creating economic sovereignty stitch by stitch. During the Abeokuta Women's Revolt of 1946-1949, organizer Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti had thousands of women wear Adire as a uniform of resistance, transforming the cloth into a wearable manifesto of solidarity.

Master artist Chief Nike Davies-Okundaye, founder of the Nike Art Centres, continues this legacy today, training thousands of young women and elevating Adire to global recognition.
